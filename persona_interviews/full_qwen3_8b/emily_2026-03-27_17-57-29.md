### Question 15

**Answer**
When players have different skill levels, the biggest issue is that the game can feel unfair or unbalanced. If someone is more experienced, they might dominate the strategies or move faster, leaving others frustrated or feeling like they’re not contributing. For example, a younger player might struggle to keep up with the pace or understand advanced blocking tactics, which could make them disengage or feel left out. The key is for the game to let everyone participate without feeling like they’re at a disadvantage, whether through adaptive mechanics, clear guidance, or flexible play modes that let players choose their comfort level. If it doesn’t address these gaps, it can turn a fun activity into a source of frustration for some.

### Question 16

**Answer**
A tutorial for a mixed-skill group should be **minimal, visual, and adaptable**—like a quick, hands-off guide that shows the core mechanics without overwhelming anyone. It should start with a short visual example, like a few moves that highlight how pieces fit, how blocking works, and the goal of claiming space. If someone needs more help, a simple “hint” button that gives a subtle nudge (like pointing out an available move or reminding them of the objective) would be useful, but it shouldn’t linger or feel like a distraction. If it’s too long or too detailed, it risks alienating someone who’s already struggling to keep up.
